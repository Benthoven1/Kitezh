Mulvium’s Vision

Beauty should be all around us.

To those who stop and smell the roses,

Mulvium is a non-profit foundation built to funnel personal wealth into global impact—incubating the creative process, commissioning master craftsmen, and restoring aspiration to public life. Its premise is simple: the vast resources accumulated in this century belong, in some measure, to beauty.

We believe in three constituencies. To the Patron, Mulvium aligns the Overton window in favor of accumulated wealth powering the frontier—wealth inexorably tied to artistic achievement. To the Artist, it redirects creative energy toward problem-solving rather than exclusively self-expression, through polymathy and humanist–technologist collaboration. To the Citizen, it will manifest and maintain the framework of social humility that society needs to reconcile divisiveness and engender harmony.

Most things that are profoundly beautiful are wrapped up in the canon of the past and in the splendor of the natural world. Why can’t we find this level of beauty in our globalized, cosmopolitan lives—in the vast art of our time?

Historically, societies made their deepest values visible. Medieval Europe externalized faith through cathedrals. Renaissance Italy materialized influence through palazzi, frescoes, and sculpture. Even industrial modernity expressed itself through monumental infrastructure—bridges and train stations—that were not merely functional, but aspirational. The built world was a mirror of what people believed mattered.

Today, the political and technological classes obfuscate beauty more than shape it. Power accumulates without leaving a legible mark on civilization. The patron class has lost its idiom. Mulvium exists to give it one back.

There needs to exist a funnel where personal wealth comes in and global impact comes out—where creative ideation and the creative process can be incubated. A non-profit foundation that directly controls real estate, castle operations, and brand—where patrons contribute their wealth. And a public benefit corporation beneath it: a subsidiary that develops the technology and media that carries this vision into daily life.

If people can’t tolerate aesthetic differences, how will they tolerate each other? Mulvium’s hope is to help the world stop and smell the roses—one artwork at a time.

With sincerity,

Mulvium

Division 01 — Vision Memo

International Festival Orchestra

To Mr. William Ackman  ·  From Mr. Benjamin T. Rossen  ·  4 April 2026

The Kitezh1 Festival Orchestra is a proposed international ensemble in residence in Cambridge, MA dedicated to illuminating and revitalizing beauty in the modern age. Given the recent transformation of Boston’s cultural scene, there is a new need for a galvanizing musical force that is mission-focused, globally-oriented, and programmatically courageous. Kitezh will present a week-long series of concerts, talks, and world premieres every September—all tackling the complex meaning and importance of aesthetic beauty. Every year will have a different theme: in September 2027, it will memorialize the 200th anniversary of Beethoven’s passing. The festival will be bookended by Beethoven’s Eroica Symphony and Missa Solemnis. Kitezh also features Reimaginings: classical and non-classical composers extend the symphonic genre by composing an original symphonic movement / tone poem that incorporates multimedia, different genres, voice, choreography, cinema, etc. We need art that intertwines craftsmanship in form with depth in content, akin to Beethoven. The orchestra will feature world-class instrumentalists across generations and backgrounds.

This non-profit festival is the first reclamation of the beautiful. However, this attention to beauty must extend beyond live music. The end goal is to reshape patronage in the arts. Contemporary art funded today, especially the entertainment industrial complex, largely overlooks beauty as a source of social cohesion. People need art that inspires deep feelings. Great art inspires the spiritual, sublime, and transcendent within us.

The first step to reshaping arts patronage is to produce and sustain this festival orchestra. An initial funding round will enable the first hires: Executive Director, Artistic Producer and Administrator, Orchestra Manager, principal musicians, and the commissioned composers for Reimaginings. It will also support my work as Artistic Director. This funding round will happen in tandem with the creation of a Board of Advisors. The Board consists of artistic luminaries and patrons who affirm the legitimacy of our operation and attract top talent—both in music and business. This is an invitation to join as the founding member of the Board of Advisors and to make a philanthropic contribution.

1 Kitezh is a placeholder name, although it illustrates an important tenet of the initiative. The original myth of Kitezh describes a 13th century Russian city that submerged in a lake following a Mongol invasion. The city remains invisible, but people still can hear the city’s celestial carillons and canticles.
